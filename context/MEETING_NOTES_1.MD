# RareJob learning material design strategy for English language learning app

### Japanese Student Learning Challenges

- Cultural barriers to English practice
  - No daily English exposure in Japan (unlike Philippines)
  - Reserved culture - students uncomfortable talking about themselves
  - Traditional education focused on textbook answers, not personal opinions
  - Students say “too tired of talking about myself” - request simulation lessons
- Structural language differences
  - Japanese sentences don’t require subjects; English always does
  - Different muscle movements needed for English vs Japanese pronunciation
  - English learning like sports - requires physical training and practice
- Conceptualization difficulties
  - Students struggle with “what to say” not “how to say it”
  - Don’t know personal interests or opinions due to educational system
  - Need content/topics to discuss in lessons for successful input-output cycle

### Current Motivation and Practice Patterns

- Primary motivators for English learning
  - Over 900 score opens job opportunities
  - Over 700 enables salary raises
- Practice constraints
  - Working adults have no time for study/hobbies
  - 5-10 minute exercises ideal for train commutes and lunch breaks
  - Students request shorter alternatives to 25-minute RareJob lessons
  - Silent practice needed for crowded trains (mumbling with masks)
- Declining motivation trends
  - Post-COVID: young people less interested in going abroad
  - Can learn everything online now
  - Korean language learning popular due to K-pop/K-drama culture

### App Development Requirements

- Must bridge to RareJob human lessons, not replace them
  - Generate personalized lessons in RareJob’s structure
  - Create mentor-side materials for tutors to use generated content
  - Students should want to discuss app content in live lessons
- Key features for Japanese market
  1. Short 5-10 minute exercises for commuting
  2. Content that gives students something to talk about
  3. Muscle movement training (pronunciation practice)
  4. Silent-friendly for train use
  5. Personalized but avoiding intellectual property issues
- Success metric: drives students back to human tutoring sessions
  - Different from Duolingo model where app satisfaction is end goal
  - Hybrid approach combining AI practice with human interaction
